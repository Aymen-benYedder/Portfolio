Can I apply for permanent residence directly through an ICT work permit?
No — the ICT is a temporary work permit, not a PR program. However, skilled Canadian work experience (TEER 0 or 1) qualifies you for Express Entry (CEC after 12 months) and Provincial Nominee Programs. Most ICT holders achieve PR within 2–4 years. The critical 2026 change: arranged employment CRS points (50–200) are removed, making PNP or category-based draws the essential new path.
What salary must be paid on an ICT work permit?
For specialized knowledge workers (C63), IRCC requires salary at or above the prevailing Canadian wage for the occupation (NOC-specific). Executives and senior managers (C62) need a wage commensurate with the role's seniority. Significantly below-market salaries trigger additional scrutiny and potential refusal.
Does my spouse get an open work permit if I hold an ICT or C10 permit?
Yes, but only if the principal worker is in a TEER 0 or 1 occupation with at least 16 months remaining on their permit at the time of the spouse's application (rules tightened January 21, 2025). A 3-year ICT permit meets this threshold immediately, but a 1-year new-office ICT (C61) likely does not. Dependent children are no longer eligible for open work permits under these measures.
Can I use the Global Skills Strategy (GSS) for faster processing on ICT or C10?
Yes. GSS offers 2-week processing for employer-specific work permits in TEER 0 or 1 occupations. LMIA-exempt workers (ICT, C10, C16) are automatically flagged for GSS if the role meets TEER criteria — no special questions needed. For LMIA-required roles, the employer must use the Global Talent Stream instead.
What happens if my ICT work permit expires before I get PR?
Options include: applying for an extension (within the 5/7-year cumulative limit), switching categories after exhausting the maximum stay with a 1-year cooling-off period, or applying for PR and a Bridging Open Work Permit (BOWP) if you have 12+ months of Canadian experience. Best practice: start your PR application 12–18 months before hitting your ICT maximum stay.

The Growing Role of LMIA-Exempt Pathways in Canada's 2026 Strategy

In 2026, Canada's International Mobility Program (IMP) targets 170,000 LMIA-exempt work permits — a 32% year-over-year increase — while the Temporary Foreign Worker Program's LMIA-required stream drops to just 60,000 permits.1 This policy pivot prioritizes agile, employer-driven exemptions over the costly Labour Market Impact Assessment for high-skilled talent. For global tech workers, two LMIA-exempt pathways dominate: the Intra-Company Transfer (ICT) under codes C61, C62, and C63, and the C10 Significant Benefit category. Both operate under R205(a) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Regulations, but they differ radically in structure, predictability, and 2026 viability.2,6

Intra-Company Transfer: C61, C62, and C63 Explained

Legal Framework and Exemption Codes

The ICT program enables multinational enterprises to transfer key personnel to Canadian affiliates without an LMIA. Since the December 2022 renumbering, three codes apply: C61 for start-ups establishing a first Canadian branch (1-year initial permit); C62 for executives and senior managers at existing entities (7-year maximum); and C63 for specialized knowledge workers (5-year maximum). The legacy code C12 was retired in 2024.2,6

Six Core Eligibility Requirements

ICT applications rest on six pillars:2,3,9

  1. Qualifying corporate relationship. Entities must be parent-subsidiary, branch, or affiliate under common ownership. Joint ventures and contractual relationships do not qualify. Documentation must include organization charts, share registers, and audited financial statements.
  2. Multinational status. The foreign enterprise must have revenue-generating operations in at least two countries (2025/2026 Fragomen guidance). Companies in a single country cannot use ICT to establish a first foreign branch.9
  3. One-year tenure rule. The employee must have been employed continuously, full-time by the foreign entity for at least one year within the three years preceding the application, in a role comparable to the Canadian position.
  4. Comparable or higher role. The Canadian position must be at the same or higher functional level. A specialized knowledge worker cannot be transferred into a generalist role.
  5. Active business. Both entities must be actively doing business. Virtual offices, home offices, and P.O. boxes are grounds for immediate refusal.
  6. Genuine Canadian business purpose. IRCC requires a real operational need — not a paper transfer designed purely for immigration benefit.

The Specialized Knowledge Trap — C63 in 2026

C63 faces the highest scrutiny among ICT streams. Knowledge must be both proprietary (unique to the company) and advanced (not widely available in Canada's labour market). Simply knowing internal software is insufficient. Applicants need at least two years with the enterprise; between one and two years triggers heightened scrutiny. Critically, applicants must demonstrate that training a Canadian replacement would cause significant business disruption, and the prevailing Canadian wage for the occupation must be met or exceeded.3,11

Duration, Processing Times, and Fees

Maximum cumulative stays: 7 years for C62, 5 years for C63, 1 year initial for C61. A one-year cooling-off period outside Canada is required once the maximum is reached. Processing times (IRCC May 26, 2026 update): US 3–5 weeks, UK 4–7 weeks, UAE 5–9 weeks, India 6–10 weeks, general online 4–12 weeks. Port-of-entry same day for visa-exempt nationals. GSS-eligible TEER 0/1 roles receive a 2-week target. Fees: CAD $230 employer compliance, CAD $155 processing, CAD $85 biometrics (if applicable).2,3,16

C10 Significant Benefit: Post-February 2026 Reality

Legal Basis and the Tightening

C10 (R205(a), officer discretion, GCMS Case Type 52) is the catch-all for foreign nationals whose work brings "significant economic, social, or cultural benefit" to Canada. On February 24, 2026, IRCC fundamentally redefined this category. C10 is now reserved strictly for "unique or exceptional situations" with quantifiable evidence of broader community or regional impact beyond the employer.4,13,14,15

The new evidentiary framework requires:4,13

  • Benefits extending beyond the applicant, their dependents, and the employer — to the broader community, region, or sector
  • A "large number" of employment or training opportunities with demonstrated impact
  • Concrete, quantifiable evidence — general claims no longer suffice
  • A formal risk assessment weighing benefits against potential harm to the Canadian labour market

The Relative Scale Clause

One important nuance: the scale of "significant" is relative to the industry, town, or sector. Creating 5 jobs might not be "large" in downtown Toronto but could qualify as significant in a small rural community. This relativity clause provides targeted flexibility.4,13

Who Should Still Consider C10 in 2026?

C10 remains viable for high-impact scenarios: researchers with quantifiable contributions to Canadian institutions, highly recognized cultural figures, technology founders demonstrating job creation and investment, scarce-skill professionals in STEM with broader community impact, and specialized medical professionals in underserved regions. For a standard tech hire — even at a senior level — C10 is no longer the appropriate route. Estimated refusal rates were 20–35% pre-2026 and are expected to rise.4,10,14

Application Process

ICT Application Roadmap

Corporate Structure Validation. Confirm the qualifying relationship with organization charts, share registers, and audited financials. The Canadian entity must be legally incorporated with a CRA Business Number and physical commercial premises.2,3

Business Plan (C61 only). Submit a 3-year plan with minimum CAD $100,000 in liquid funds for first-year costs, a realistic staffing plan, and a physical commercial lease.2

Employer Portal Submission. The Canadian entity submits an Offer of Employment via the IRCC Employer Portal with business activities, job duties, wage, and exemption code. The CAD $230 compliance fee is paid here, generating an Offer of Employment number.2,3

Worker Application. The foreign national submits IMM 1295 online, at a Port of Entry (visa-exempt nationals), or from inside Canada. Fees: CAD $155 processing plus CAD $85 biometrics where applicable.2

Supporting Documents. Corporate documents (incorporation certificates, financial statements, commercial lease, business license) and worker documents (employment history letter proving the 1-year tenure, pay stubs, detailed job descriptions for both roles, CV, education credentials, and — critical for C63 — training logs, unique certifications, and proprietary methodology documentation).2,11

C10-Specific Evidence (Post-Feb 2026). Beyond standard documentation, C10 now requires quantifiable benefit projections (job numbers, investment amounts), third-party corroboration (industry letters, news coverage, government endorsements), evidence of uniqueness demonstrating irreplaceability, broader community impact documentation, risk-mitigation statements, and support letters from industry associations or economic development agencies.4,13,14

PR Pathways — The 2026 Landscape Shift

ICT → Permanent Residence

ICT work permits are temporary but provide an excellent PR runway. The skilled Canadian work experience (TEER 0 or 1) qualifies holders for Canadian Experience Class (CEC) after 12 months, Federal Skilled Worker Program, and Provincial Nominee Programs. PNP nomination adds 600 CRS points, guaranteeing an Invitation to Apply.8,12

The critical 2025/2026 change: IRCC removed the 50-point (TEER 0) and 200-point (TEER 00) arranged employment CRS bonuses from Express Entry. ICT and C10 holders can no longer rely on job offer points. The new optimal PR strategy requires higher language scores (target CLB 9+), spousal improvements, French-language ability (French-category draws at CRS ~393), PNP nomination (600 points), or STEM/French category-based draws. Typical timeline: ICT → 1 year Canadian experience → Express Entry/PNP → PR in 2–4 years total.8,12

The Start-Up Visa Pause and Alternatives

SUV program paused January 1, 2026. Valid 2025 commitment certificate holders must apply by June 30, 2026. Queued applications face 3.3–4.3 years processing. A new entrepreneur pilot is expected in 2026. Interim alternatives: C11 owner-operator or provincial entrepreneur streams (Ontario OINP CAD $200K–$600K, BC PNP Entrepreneur CAD $200K+, Manitoba MPNP CAD $250K+, Saskatchewan SINP CAD $300K+).5,17

Top Refusal Reasons and Mitigation

ICT Refusals

Estimated approval rates: C62/C63 at 75–85% for well-prepared files; C61 at 60–70%. Top refusal reasons:3,11

  1. Specialized knowledge not genuinely specialized. Mitigation: document proprietary nature explicitly with training logs, unique certifications, and evidence of business disruption without the employee.
  2. Corporate relationship not established. Mitigation: comprehensive org charts, share registers, audited financials, and a clear relationship narrative.
  3. Canadian entity not actively doing business. Mitigation: payroll, client contracts, invoices, commercial lease, active bank account.
  4. Less than 1 year prior employment. Mitigation: precise documentation of continuous full-time employment.
  5. Role not a qualifying category. Mitigation: detailed job descriptions matching TEER 0/1; avoid generalist language.

C10 Refusals

Post-February 2026, the top reasons are: benefit not demonstrable (vague assertions without concrete evidence), not "unique or exceptional" (officer determines routine hire), benefit limited to employer only, temporary intent not established, and adverse immigration history.4,13

Conclusion

Canada's 2026 LMIA-exempt landscape presents two diverging pathways. The Intra-Company Transfer program (C61/C62/C63) remains a robust, structured route for multinational tech talent — offering 2-week GSS processing, up to 7 years of stay, and a clear PR trajectory via Express Entry or PNP. The primary risk is the C63 specialized knowledge trap, demanding rigorous documentation of proprietary expertise.

The C10 Significant Benefit category was fundamentally restricted in February 2026. It is no longer a flexible LMIA alternative for routine tech hires but is reserved for genuinely exceptional contributors with third-party-verified community impact and substantially higher refusal risk.

The removal of arranged employment CRS points reshaped PR strategy for all LMIA-exempt holders. The new optimal path combines skilled Canadian work experience with PNP nomination (600 points), French-language category draws, or maximized language and education scores. PR planning must begin on Day 1 of any work permit — not after arrival.
